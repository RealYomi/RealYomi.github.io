From Raw Data to Variogram Models:
Quantifying Spatial Variability in Engineering Fields

In the previous post, we built a covariance matrix from a chosen variogram model and used it to decompose a spatially varying material property field via the Karhunen–Loève expansion. We answered the question: given a variogram, what do we do with it?

This post takes a step back and answers the question that comes before that: where does the variogram come from?

In practice, you do not pick a variogram from thin air. You measure it from real data (experimental measurements of a material property at known spatial locations) and then fit a theoretical model to those measurements. That fitted model is what feeds into the covariance matrix.

Here, we walk through that process end to end: from raw spatial data, to an experimental variogram, to a ranked set of fitted theoretical models. The workflow is entirely material-agnostic and applies to any spatially varying scalar field you can measure in 2D, such as:

  • Elastic modulus or hardness mapped across a metal alloy specimen
  • Ultrasonic pulse velocity (UPV) over a concrete slab
  • Porosity or permeability of a geomaterial core sample
  • Thermal conductivity of a composite panel
  • Surface roughness of an additively manufactured part

The data and scripts for this post are available on GitHub.

Why Variograms?

Before we compute anything, it is worth asking: why go through this trouble?

A variogram is the standard tool for characterizing how a spatial property varies with distance. It tells you two critical things at once:

  1. The range — beyond what separation distance do two measurements become practically uncorrelated?
  2. The sill — what is the overall variance of the field?

Together, these two numbers encode the spatial "personality" of your material field. Without them, any stochastic simulation of that field is just guesswork.

The Dataset

For illustrative purposes, the experimental data used in this post consists of a 2D spatial map of a material property field (unit masonry brick dynamic modulus in GPa), (z), sampled at a discrete grid of locations defined by coordinates (x) and (y). The three arrays — x_cor.xlsx, y_cor.xlsx, and z_data.xlsx — are available in the GitHub repository.

Spatial map of a material property field

Figure 1 (left): 2D heat map of the material property field. Figure 1 (right): 3D surface and contour plot.

In our case the field is standardized before variogram computation, meaning the data have been transformed to have zero mean and unit variance. This standardization is important: it makes the variogram, and any model fitted to it, dimensionless and transferable across materials and scales.

Step 1 — The Experimental Variogram

What Is a Variogram?

The experimental (empirical) variogram, also called the semivariogram, is defined as:

$$ \hat{\gamma}(h) = \frac{1}{2|N(h)|} \sum_{(i,j) \in N(h)} \left[ z(\mathbf{x}_i) - z(\mathbf{x}_j) \right]^2 $$

where:

  • \(h\) is the lag distance — the separation between two measurement points
  • \(N(h)\) is the set of all point pairs separated by approximately \(h\), within a chosen tolerance
  • \(|N(h)|\) is the number of such pairs

In plain terms: for every pair of data points, we compute the squared difference in their property values, group those pairs by how far apart they are, and average. The result is a curve showing how dissimilarity grows with distance.

A few key features to look for in the resulting curve:

Figure 2: Key Variogram Features

Feature Symbol Meaning
Nugget \(C_0\) Variogram value at \(h \to 0\); reflects measurement noise or micro-scale variability
Sill \(C_0 + C\) Plateau value the variogram levels off to; equals the total variance
Range \(a\) Lag distance at which the sill is reached; beyond this, points are uncorrelated

How We Compute It

The computation involves three user-defined parameters:

  • Lag step \(h\): the width of each distance bin
  • Lag tolerance: how loosely we define "approximately \(h\)" (typically 0 to 0.5)
  • Number of directions and angular tolerance: for directional (anisotropic) variograms

For an isotropic assumption (where spatial correlation is the same in all directions) we ignore the directional breakdown and aggregate all pairs at a given lag distance. This is the most common starting point in industrial practice.

For an anisotropic analysis, we compute directional variograms (e.g., 0°, 45°, 90°, 135°) and check whether the range differs between directions. If it does, the field has a preferred orientation of correlation, a useful insight for material characterization.

The Python Script — Experimental Variogram

The full script is available in the GitHub repository. Below is an annotated summary of the key steps.

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# --- Load spatial coordinates and property values ---
x = pd.read_excel(r'C:\x_cor.xlsx', header=None)
y = pd.read_excel(r'C:\y_cor.xlsx', header=None)
z = pd.read_excel(r'C:\z_data.xlsx', header=None)

# --- Call the variogram function ---
# The function prompts for: lag step, lag tolerance,
# number of directions, angular tolerance, and initial direction.
results = varioTry1T2(x, y, z)

Internally, the function:

  1. Builds a meshgrid from x and y, then flattens the coordinates and property values.
  2. Computes pairwise distances and angles for the upper-triangular matrix of all point pairs.
  3. Bins the squared differences by lag distance and direction into aggregated semivariance values.
  4. Plots both directional and isotropic variograms on the same figure.

The output results is a NumPy array storing the variogram values (lag distance, semivariance, pair count, standard deviation) organized by direction, suitable for export to Excel for the model-fitting step.

Figure 3: Anisotropic & Isotropic experimental variogram computed from the spatial material property data. The x-axis is lag distance \(h\); the y-axis is semivariance \(\hat{\gamma}(h)\).

A note on pair counts: Following established geostatistical practice, only lags with \(N(h)\) \(\geq\) \(0.5\%\) of the maximum pair count were retained — lags below this threshold carry too few pairs to produce reliable semivariance estimates and can disproportionately skew model fitting. Remaining lags were down-weighted during fitting using weights proportional to \(N(h)/\hat{\gamma}(h)^2\), the Cressie weighting scheme, to account for residual heteroscedasticity. This is implemented directly in the fitting script via the weights_cressie array in Step 2.

Step 2 — Fitting a Theoretical Model

An experimental variogram is just a scatter of points. To use it in downstream computations, that is building a covariance matrix, simulating random fields, we need a smooth, positive-definite theoretical model \(\gamma(h)\) that is fitted to those points.

Candidate Models

We test seven standard variogram models from the gstools library. All share the same structural anatomy: a nugget \(C_0\), a sill \(\sigma^2\), and a correlation length \(\ell\). Some have an additional shape parameter \(\alpha\).

Model Formula
Exponential \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2\left[1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{h}{\ell}\right)\right]\)
Gaussian \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2\left[1 - \exp\!\left(-\frac{h^2}{\ell^2}\right)\right]\)
Spherical \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2\left[\frac{3h}{2\ell} - \frac{h^3}{2\ell^3}\right],\ h \leq \ell\)
Stable \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2\left[1 - \exp\!\left(-\left(\frac{h}{\ell}\right)^\alpha\right)\right]\)
Rational \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2\left[1 - \left(1 + \frac{h^2}{\ell^2}\right)^{-\alpha}\right]\)
Circular \(\gamma(h) = \sigma^2 \left[1 - \frac{2}{\pi} \arccos\!\left(\frac{h}{\ell}\right) + \frac{2h}{\pi\ell}\sqrt{1 - \frac{h^2}{\ell^2}}\right]\)
Cubic \(\gamma(h)\) polynomial, smooth at the origin

The Exponential model implies a rough field (non-differentiable). The Gaussian implies a very smooth, highly correlated field. The Stable and Rational models interpolate between these extremes via \(\alpha\). The Spherical and Circular are both compact-support models, they reach their sill exactly at \(h = \ell\) and assign zero correlation beyond it, with the Circular approaching the sill slightly more gradually. In practice, the data decide, which is exactly why we fit and rank all of them.

Note the connection to the previous post: once a model is selected, its parameters (\(\sigma^2\), \(\ell\), \(\alpha\)) directly define the covariance function via:

$$ C(h) = C(0) - \gamma(h) $$

which is then used to assemble the full covariance matrix for the Karhunen–Loève expansion.

The Python Script — Model Fitting

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import gstools as gs
from matplotlib import pyplot as plt

# --- STEP 1: Import desire experimental variogram data from results ---
# i:e results = varioTry1T2(x, y, z) from the previouse Python script ---
bin_center = results[239:321, 0]   # lag distances
gamma_exp  = results[239:321, 1]   # semivariance values
count_fit  = results[239:321, 3]   # pair counts

# --- STEP 2: Compute weights ---
### Cressie weighting scheme ###
# w(h) = N(h) / γ(h)²  -- down-weights noisy, high-semivariance points
# Add small epsilon to avoid division by zero
epsilon = 1e-10
weights_cressie = count_fit / (gamma_exp**2 + epsilon)

# Normalize weights for numerical stability (optional but recommended)
weights_cressie = weights_cressie / weights_cressie.max()

### Alternative: Use GSTools built-in weighting options: ###
# weights = "count"   # uses N(h)
# weights = "sqrt"    # uses sqrt(N(h))
# weights = "exp"     # uses N(h)/γ(h)² (Cressie) -- SAME as custom!

# --- STEP 3: Define candidate models ---
models = {
    "Exponential": gs.Exponential,
    "Stable"     : gs.Stable,
    "Gaussian"   : gs.Gaussian,
    "Spherical"  : gs.Spherical,
    "Rational"   : gs.Rational,
    "Circular"   : gs.Circular,
    "Cubic"      : gs.Cubic,
}
scores = {}

# --- STEP 4: Fit models and collect results ---
plt.scatter(bin_center, gamma_exp, color="k", label="data", s=3)
axes = plt.gca()
results_list = []

# Fit all models with WEIGHTED LEAST SQUARES
for i, model_name in enumerate(models):

    # Create model instance
    fit_model = models[model_name](dim=2)

    # FIT WITH WEIGHTS: Use Cressie weights (custom array)
    # Option A: Custom Cressie weights
    para, pcov, r2 = fit_model.fit_variogram(
        bin_center, 
        gamma_exp, 
        weights=weights_cressie,      
        return_r2=True, 
        nugget=True                   # Allow nugget estimation
    )

    # # Option B: Use built-in GSTools weighting (simpler, nearly equivalent)
    # para, pcov, r2 = fit_model.fit_variogram(
    #     bin_center, gamma_exp, 
    #     weights="exp",              # "exp" = Cressie weighting internally
    #     return_r2=True, 
    #     nugget=True
    # )

    # Extract parameters safely
    var_value = para.get("var", np.nan)
    len_scale_value = para.get("len_scale", np.nan)
    nugget_value = para.get("nugget", 0.0)

    # Plot fitted model
    fit_model.plot(x_max=41, ax=axes)
    results_list.append({
        'Model': model_name, 'Covariance_Matrix': pcov, 'R_squared': r2, **para
    })
    scores[model_name] = r2
results_df = pd.DataFrame(results_list)

# --- STEP 5: Results & Rank by pseudo-R² ---
ranking = sorted(scores.items(), key=lambda item: item[1], reverse=True)

print("\n" + "="*60)
print("MODEL RANKING by Weighted Pseudo-R² Score")
print("="*60)
for i, (model, score) in enumerate(ranking, 1):
    print(f"{i:>3}. {model:>12}: R² = {score:.5f}")

# --- STEP 6: Plot ---
plt.xlabel('Lag distance (h-cm)', fontsize=14)
plt.ylabel(r'Gamma (Semivariogram) $\gamma(h)$', fontsize=14)
plt.yticks(fontsize=12)
plt.xticks(fontsize=12)
axes.grid(True)
sorted_models = [m for m, _ in ranking]
fitted_legends = [f"{m} (R² = {scores[m]:.3f})" for m in sorted_models]
handles, labels = axes.get_legend_handles_labels()
plt.legend(handles,
           ["Experimental variogram (isotropic)"] + fitted_legends,
           loc='lower right')
plt.show()

The fit_variogram method minimizes the weighted least-squares distance between the experimental points and the theoretical curve, returning the fitted parameters, the parameter covariance matrix pcov, and the pseudo-R² score r2. Figure 4 is an Isotropic experimental variogram (black dots) with all seven candidate theoretical models fitted. Models are ranked by pseudo-R² in the legend.

Figure 4: Isotropic experimental variogram (black dots) with all seven candidate theoretical models fitted.

On the choice of isotropy fitting here: Moderate degree of anisotropy were observed from the Directional (Anisotropy) variograms. However, the isotropic variogram was retained for model fitting, as the primary objective is to establish a baseline spatial correlation structure suitable for covariance matrix construction. In applications where directional dependence is critical, such as oriented fibre composites or laminated structures, an anisotropic model should be pursued instead.

Ranking and Selection

The pseudo-R² score used here is:

$$ R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i \left[\hat{\gamma}(h_i) - \gamma_{\text{model}}(h_i)\right]^2}{\sum_i \left[\hat{\gamma}(h_i) - \bar{\gamma}\right]^2} $$

A score of 1.0 is a perfect fit; a score below 0 means the model is worse than simply using the mean. In practice, no universal threshold exists for spatial data, acceptable fit depends on data density, measurement noise, and the intended downstream application. The ranking is therefore used comparatively: the best-fitting model among the candidates is selected, with the absolute R² value serving as a secondary diagnostic rather than a hard acceptance criterion.

The ranking step is not merely cosmetic. Different models impose different assumptions about the smoothness of the underlying field. Selecting the wrong model; say, a Gaussian for a rough field, can lead to a covariance matrix that produces unrealistically smooth simulated fields and, downstream, overly optimistic structural reliability estimates.

Interpretation of Results

Once a best-fitting model is identified, the key outputs to record are:

  • Variance \(\sigma^2\): the sill of the standardized variogram (should be ≈ 1.0 for standardized data)
  • Correlation length \(\ell\): the spatial scale over which the property is strongly correlated (i.e directly controls the dominant mode shapes in the KLE)
  • Nugget \(C_0\): if non-zero, signals either measurement noise or sub-grid-scale variability
  • Shape parameter \(\alpha\) (for Stable/Rational): controls field smoothness

These parameters connect directly to the covariance matrix construction described in the previous post. In that post, we used a Rational model with \(\sigma^2 = 1.0\), \(\ell = 3.0\), \(\alpha = 1.14\) (parameters that would emerge naturally from the fitting workflow described here).

Conclusion

Variogram analysis is the essential first step in any stochastic modeling workflow that involves spatially varying material properties. By combining:

  • an experimental variogram computed from measured spatial data
  • a suite of fitted theoretical models ranked by goodness of fit

we obtain a compact, physically meaningful description of the spatial variability in a material field (regardless of the specific material or application).

This description then feeds directly into the covariance matrix, the Karhunen–Loève expansion, and ultimately into stochastic finite element simulations and uncertainty quantification workflows.
